Competition Day: PID control

Drag Race

time: 11.27(best); rank: 3rd

S:85:; P:40; I:1; D:48.

We max our speed to 100.

We choose a P value at about 10 since the line is fairly straight; a small I value gives us small settling time; and a small D value which enables stability.

Loop Track

loops: 2; rank: n/a

S:30; P:38; I:10; D:53.

Our robot is not function at this time because we just switched to the new battery, so we have a low speed to ensure it is going.

We choose a P value at about 38 so it will be sensitive enough for the curves in the loop without developing too much overshoot. A small I value gives us small settling time, and a decent D value eliminates the overshoot created by the P value.

Frequency Sweep

Distance: 4.5; time: 5min; rank: n/a; Loop: n/a

S:46; P:38; I:1; D:50.

At this time, the battery have been worn from the drag race, so we use a speed of 46, which is actually slower than the loop circuit, so the car will have more time to react.

We choose a P value at about 38 so it will be very sensitive at the low speed.

We increased the D value so the car will not have too much overshoot.

Reflection

Poster:

During the last week, we think that our photoresistors are not neatly aligned, so we resoldered the new photoresistors.

We feel that the back of wheels are not having enough friction on the track, so we add rubber band to each of the wheel.

We also added weight to the back of the car and on the motors, so the motor can perform better.

If we have enough time, I hope that we can find a better PID after we switch out the battery. We have been always testing it on a lower voltage, and at a high voltage, it stops performing as we wanted.
